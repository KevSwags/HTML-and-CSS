About me

Growing up in a low-income Tanzanian family, my parents taught me to maintain high standards for my academic and professional achievements amidst the intangible hierarchical impediments that accompany my identity. I continue to hold myself to these standards as a Computer Science and Economics student all while recognizing the ways in which I can utilize my own privileges to help others who may not have the same access as I do. I have been able to use the theoretical knowledge I have acquired in the classroom in a practical way that has tangible effects on Tanzanians living in the U.S. I am of the strong belief that as a young Black Man from a low-income household, interested in a career within the technology industry, the onus is on me to ensure that technology leaves no one behind. Being involved in local communities now, allows me to ensure that I center small community needs in my future work as a software engineer and web developer. I am often hesitant to recount stories of my childhood, in an attempt not to perpetuate a harmful narrative of the stereotypically undeveloped Africa—i.e. the times when my parents biked with me to the nearest hospital 20 km away from home. During my time at Queens College, I hope to apply for fellowship opportunities that will put me well on my way to achieving my goals of becoming a web developer and software engineer to increase access and equity for New York City communities.
